# Day 28: Final Reflection + The Strategic Brief

## The Idea

You've spent four weeks on this. Foundations. Implementation. People. Scale. That's the full arc of AI strategy as it actually plays out in real organizations — not the vendor-pitched version, not the LinkedIn-content version, but the version where you have to make real decisions with real budget for real people.

Today is the synthesis day. Your assignment is to consolidate everything into one document — a Strategic AI Brief — that becomes the foundation for the next year of work. Not a slide deck. A real document, written by you, that says what you've decided and why.

If you've done the work along the way, the document already mostly exists. You wrote a strategy brief in Week 1. A pilot charter in Week 2. A people memo in Week 3. Today we tie those three into a single coherent strategic narrative that someone — your CEO, your board, your team, your future self — can read and understand exactly where you stand and what comes next.

This document is the test of whether the four weeks actually changed anything. If you can write it, you've internalized the discipline. If you can't, you've consumed information without applying it. The doing is the point.

## The Final Strategic Brief

Here's the structure. Aim for 4-6 pages total. It's a real working document, not an executive summary.

### Section 1: Where We Are (current state)

Three subsections, one paragraph each:

**Strategic context.** What are the top 3 business priorities? Where does AI fit (or doesn't fit) into how we win? What did we believe about AI four weeks ago that we now believe differently?

**Operational reality.** How many AI initiatives are active, in what stages? What's our portfolio's honest ROI today? What's our talent inventory? What's our biggest organizational gap?

**Risk surface.** What's our exposure on the five ethics practices (Day 18)? What pending regulatory or reputational risks do we have? What's our kill-switch readiness?

This section should be uncomfortable to write. If it's not, you're not being honest with yourself. The accurate current state is almost always less impressive than the marketing version.

### Section 2: Where We're Going (12-month plan)

Three subsections:

**Strategic priorities for AI in the next 12 months.** No more than three. What's the AI work that, if it succeeds, materially advances the business? What's the AI work that, if it fails, materially hurts? Be specific about both.

**Portfolio plan.** Which existing initiatives advance, which get killed, which get scoped down? Use the four-stage framework from Day 22. List specific projects with their disposition.

**Capability investments.** What are you investing in *capability* building (Day 27) that won't pay back this year but compounds over the decade? Be explicit. Most plans skip this and lose the decade.

### Section 3: How We're Going to Get There (operating system)

Three subsections:

**People.** What's our 12-month plan across the three tiers (Day 16)? Specific hires, specific upskilling investments, specific organizational changes. Reference the Week 3 memo.

**Operating model.** What's our intake-to-production process (Day 25)? Where are the bottlenecks? What single change in operating model would most accelerate our work?

**Governance and ethics.** What's our minimum viable governance (Day 05) and how is it scaling with the portfolio? What's the next-most-important investment in the five ethics practices?

### Section 4: The Strategic Choice We're Making

This is the hardest paragraph in the document. In one paragraph, articulate the strategic choice you're making — what kind of AI organization you're trying to build, and what you're explicitly choosing not to do to get there.

If you can't write this paragraph, you don't yet have an AI strategy. You have an AI activity list. The choice is the strategy.

### Section 5: How We'll Know If We're Winning

Three to five concrete metrics, each with a current baseline and a 12-month target. Not "improve AI maturity by 30%." Specific things like:

- *"3 AI systems in production with documented business outcomes by Q3"*
- *"Cost per AI initiative shipped reduced by 25% by Q4"*
- *"AI-fluent training completed for 80% of mid-level managers by year-end"*
- *"Community of Practice with 100+ active members and 12 user-generated case studies"*

Tie each to a person who's accountable. If no one's accountable for the metric, it won't move.

## The Self-Assessment

Once the brief is drafted, run through these honest questions:

**1. Could a smart outsider read this and understand exactly what we're doing?**

If your brief reads like a consultant deck full of jargon and no decisions, rewrite it in plain language. Real strategy can be explained to your most thoughtful family member in one sitting.

**2. Does it say no to anything?**

Strategy is choosing what NOT to do. If your brief says yes to everything — every use case, every initiative, every direction — it's not a strategy. It's a wishlist.

**3. Is the budget reality reflected?**

If your brief assumes 3x the budget you actually have, it's fiction. Resource constraints are part of the strategy, not afterthoughts.

**4. Have you been honest about risk?**

Most strategic briefs underweight risk. Make sure the failure modes — talent loss, regulatory shifts, vendor consolidation, internal political collapse — are explicitly acknowledged with response plans.

**5. Could you defend this to a hostile board?**

Imagine a board member who's skeptical of AI investment. Could your brief survive their questions? If not, what's the weakest section?

## Common Patterns at the End of Four Weeks

Four archetypes of where people land after this series. Recognizing yours helps focus the next year.

**"I have a strategy now, but my organization isn't ready to execute on it."**
The most common pattern. Your thinking is sharper than your operational capacity. The next 6-12 months are about building the operating muscle — talent, processes, governance — to execute on the strategy you've articulated. Day 16, 19, 25 are your reread material.

**"I have a strategy but I don't have the political capital to push it."**
You see what needs to happen. Your CEO doesn't, or your business unit leaders are skeptical. The next 6-12 months are about coalition-building — Day 11 (stakeholders) and Day 17 (change management). The strategy doesn't matter if you can't get organizational alignment.

**"I have execution but no strategy."**
You're shipping AI. You're getting wins. But there's no underlying strategic frame — you're being pulled by opportunity, not driven by a thesis. Your work the next 6 months is to retroactively articulate the strategy that explains your wins, then use it to filter the next year's portfolio. Day 01 + Day 22 are your priorities.

**"I'm at the beginning."**
You read this series, did some of the exercises, but haven't yet shipped much real AI work. That's fine — you're building the muscle. The next 12 months are about getting to your first production system using the discipline you've internalized. Day 04 (prioritization) and Day 08 (pilot design) are your starting points.

## The 100-Day Plan

Beyond the strategic brief, write a separate 100-day plan: the specific, named, dated actions you're taking to advance the strategy in the next quarter. No more than 10 items. Each with an owner, a date, and a definition of done.

The brief is the strategic frame. The 100-day plan is the operational instantiation. Without both, the strategy stays aspirational.

## A Final Note

Strategy is a discipline, not a deliverable. The brief you write today is your version 1.0. In 90 days, you'll know more — and the brief should evolve. In 12 months, the strategy may need to change shape entirely as the AI landscape shifts.

The companies that win the AI decade aren't the ones with the best strategy in 2025. They're the ones who treat strategy as an ongoing practice — revisited quarterly, refined annually, adjusted in response to learning. They build the discipline, not the document.

The discipline shows up in five practices that compound over years:

- **Honest current-state assessment** (the hardest one — most organizations lie to themselves)
- **Strategic choice** (saying no to things, even tempting things)
- **Patient capital allocation** (Day 27 — the long-game investments most organizations cut first)
- **Operating discipline** (Day 25 — the boring work that determines whether anything ships)
- **Continuous learning** (Day 21 — codifying institutional knowledge so the next decision is informed by the last 50)

If you keep these five practices going, you don't need any specific framework. The frameworks become aids; the practices become the strategy.

## Your Move

Write your Final Strategic AI Brief. Use the structure above. 4-6 pages. Take 2-3 hours.

Share it with three people: an executive sponsor, a peer who'll push back honestly, and one person who's affected by the work but hasn't been deeply involved in shaping it. Their three sets of feedback give you a triangulated read on what's clear, what's not, and what you're missing.

Schedule a calendar reminder for 90 days from now to revisit the brief. Update it. Document what's changed and why. That's the discipline that compounds.

You've finished the 30-day series. The work begins now.

Mintzberg argues strategy is emergent and adaptive — which is exactly what AI strategy needs to be.
- [Richard Rumelt, "Good Strategy / Bad Strategy"](https://www.harpercollins.com/products/good-strategy-bad-strategy-richard-rumelt) — The book that names the difference. Most "AI strategy" documents are bad strategy by Rumelt's definition. After 28 days of work, you should be able to write good strategy.
