# Day 20: Building an AI Community of Practice

## The Idea

Here's a phenomenon I've watched repeatedly: a company invests heavily in AI training, builds a strong central ML team, ships a few successful pilots, and then... the energy plateaus. The AI work stays inside the team that built it. The rest of the organization has been "trained" but doesn't actually do anything different. New AI use cases stop emerging. The flywheel stalls.

The missing piece is almost always the same: there's no mechanism for people across the organization to learn from each other, share what's working, and pull AI into their own work without having to go through the central team for permission.

That mechanism has a name. It's called a Community of Practice — and the AI version of it is one of the highest-leverage investments most organizations skip.

A Community of Practice (CoP) is not a Slack channel. It's not a quarterly all-hands. It's not an internal newsletter. Those are artifacts of one. The actual thing is a self-sustaining group of practitioners — across departments, mostly volunteers — who learn from each other, share resources, and elevate each other's work over time.

When it works, it's the difference between AI being a corporate initiative and AI being a movement.

## In Practice

A 3,000-person professional services firm shipped its first three AI use cases in 2024. The central AI team was strong — six people, well-resourced, capable. But by mid-2025, they were the bottleneck for every new idea. Anyone in the firm with an AI use case had to queue up for the team's attention. Most ideas died in the queue.

The chief data officer launched what she called an "AI Practitioners' Guild." It was modest at first: an opt-in email list, a monthly hour-long virtual meeting, and a shared library of prompts and case studies on the company intranet. She found 14 people from across the firm — analysts, mid-level managers, a couple of paralegals, two accountants — who'd been independently experimenting with AI tools and had something to teach.

The first meeting was awkward. Nobody knew what was supposed to happen. She structured it: 10 minutes of "what worked this month" from three volunteers, 20 minutes of an open question from the group, 30 minutes of demos and Q&A. People left tentatively engaged.

Six months later, the Guild had 180 members. The monthly meeting had moved to Zoom because the room was too small. Members had built and shared 40+ prompt templates, run their own micro-pilots, and started training their own teams. The central AI team's queue was shorter — not because demand had dropped, but because lower-stakes use cases were being handled in the field by Guild members.

Eighteen months later, the Guild had 600 members. It had spawned three sub-communities (legal AI, finance AI, client engagement AI). Two Guild members had transferred into the central AI team. Three had been promoted in their existing roles specifically because of their AI work. The CIO presented the Guild as one of the firm's competitive advantages in client pitches.

Total budget for the Guild over 18 months: about $80K, mostly in time-not-billed for the founder and a small honorarium for guest speakers. ROI: incalculable, but clearly the highest-leverage AI investment the firm had made.

## What Makes a CoP Actually Work

Most "communities of practice" launched with a Slack channel and good intentions die quietly within six months. The ones that thrive share five characteristics:

**1. A founder with both credibility and time.**

Not a sponsor. Not a steering committee. A *founder* — one person who is identified as the human face of the community for at least its first year, who is publicly committed to it, and who has 10-15% of their time formally allocated to making it work.

This person needs technical credibility (so members trust them), organizational credibility (so leadership backs them), and stamina (so they don't burn out in month four). The founder role can transition to others later, but in year one, it's a single accountable human.

**2. A regular cadence — same time, same shape.**

Monthly is the right cadence for most organizations. Weekly is exhausting. Quarterly loses momentum. The meeting needs to be at the same time each month (e.g., "second Wednesday at noon") and have the same shape so people know what to expect.

A working pattern: 10 minutes of community announcements, 15 minutes of "what worked this month" from 2-3 volunteers, 20 minutes of guest speaker or deep-dive on a topic, 15 minutes of open Q&A. Total: 60 minutes. Recordings posted within 24 hours.

**3. Volunteers, not draftees.**

The members have to want to be there. Mandatory CoPs don't work — they become attendance-checkbox exercises that produce no learning. The founder's job is to create a community attractive enough that people opt in, then get out of the way and let it self-sustain.

Counter-intuitively, the right metric in the first year is not member count. It's member *engagement* — the number of people who contribute, share, demo, or volunteer to speak. A 30-person community with 8 active contributors beats a 300-person community with 3.

**4. Shared artifacts that compound over time.**

The community needs a place to store and accumulate the things its members produce — prompt templates, case studies, post-mortems, recorded sessions, reading recommendations. As that library grows, it becomes a reason for new members to join (the value is already there) and a reason for existing members to stay (their contributions are visible and recognized).

This can be a Notion workspace, an internal wiki, a SharePoint site, a GitHub-style repo. The platform matters less than the discipline of actually maintaining it. Most CoPs fail at this — the library decays into a dumping ground of stale links nobody curates.

**5. Air cover from leadership.**

The CoP needs visible support from a senior executive — not a sponsor in name, an *active* sponsor who shows up to meetings occasionally, references the community in their own all-hands talks, and ensures members aren't penalized for the time they spend on it.

Without this air cover, the community becomes risky for members ("am I supposed to be doing this on company time?"). With it, the community becomes a career-development opportunity that managers actively encourage.

## Framework

**The CoP Maturity Curve:**

```
   Member
   Engagement
       ▲
       │
       │                                    *** Self-sustaining
       │                            *  *  *
       │                      *  *
       │                  *
       │              *
       │           *  Critical mass
       │         *      (~50-100 active)
       │       *
       │     *
       │    *      Founder driven
       │   *       (~10-30 members)
       │  *
       │ *      Awkward beginning
       │*       (5-15 members)
       │
       └─────────────────────────────────────────► Time
       Month 0      Month 6       Month 12      Month 18+
```

Most CoPs die in months 2-5 — the awkward beginning. The energy is artificial, the meetings feel forced, and the founder can't see the path to critical mass. **The founder's main job in months 2-5 is to keep showing up consistently when it doesn't yet feel worth it.**

If you make it past month 6 with even a small core of regular contributors, you've usually crossed the discontinuity. The community begins to feel like its own thing rather than a thing the founder is hauling along.

By month 12-18, if you've done it well, the community is bigger than any one person — including the founder. That's the goal. A community dependent on one person isn't a community; it's a fan club.

## What to Watch For

**The "executive showcase" failure.** Some companies use the CoP as a stage for senior executives to talk about AI. This kills it. The CoP is for practitioners learning from each other, not for leadership broadcasting. Executives can attend and observe; they shouldn't dominate.

**The "vendor pitch" failure.** Once a community has 100+ members, AI vendors will figure out it exists and offer to "speak." Resist. Vendor-heavy programming makes the community feel like a sales channel and erodes trust. If you must include vendors, treat them like guest faculty with strict content guidelines.

**The "central team takeover" failure.** Sometimes the central AI team starts using the CoP as a project-distribution mechanism — assigning work to members, pulling them into central initiatives. This drains the community of energy because members are no longer self-directing. The CoP and the central team should be peers, not boss-and-staff.

## Hot Take

**Hot take:** The right time to launch your AI Community of Practice is *before* you have your first AI win, not after. Most organizations wait until they have something to talk about. By then, the central AI team is established, the bottleneck has set in, and the cultural pattern of "AI happens over there" is already concrete.

The companies that build durable AI capability launch the community early — even when meetings are 6 people and 4 of them are awkwardly nodding. The early community shapes how the organization absorbs AI. Late communities try to redirect a culture that's already formed.

## Your Move

If your organization has more than 500 people and any kind of formal AI work happening, you should have an AI Community of Practice. Today's exercise:

1. Identify the founder. Who has the credibility, time (10-15%), and stamina for this? It's probably not you if you're a senior leader — find a respected mid-level practitioner.
2. Get explicit air cover. Have a 30-minute conversation with a senior sponsor about what they'll do publicly to support the community.
3. Set the first meeting. Pick a date six weeks out. Set a calendar invite. Write a one-paragraph description of what the community is and isn't.
4. Identify the first 8-10 members. Direct outreach beats broadcast invitations. Find the people already doing AI work in their corner of the company.
5. Define the first three meeting topics. Have content ready so the early meetings don't feel empty.

If you do this within 30 days, you'll have a functioning community within six months. If you don't, you'll be reading this post again 18 months from now wondering why your AI flywheel stalled.

## Go Deeper

- [Etienne Wenger, "Communities of Practice: Learning, Meaning, and Identity"](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/communities-of-practice/F22A01A4F7E0E5E1B6F3D1B1F5F5F5F5) — The foundational text on the topic. Dense but worth the time. Most contemporary CoP guidance traces back to Wenger's work in the 1990s.
- [Spotify Engineering Culture](https://engineering.atspotify.com/2014/03/27/spotify-engineering-culture-part-1/) — The "Spotify model" gets oversold, but the chapters on Guilds and Chapters describe one of the more successful applied CoP structures in tech. Worth reading even though Spotify itself has moved on from parts of it.
- [HBR, "Cultivating Communities of Practice"](https://hbr.org/2000/01/communities-of-practice-the-organizational-frontier) — The classic management-theory framing, with practical advice on launching and sustaining CoPs in corporate environments. Old (2000) but still useful.
