## The People Audit

This week's reflection has a different shape than Week 1 or Week 2. Those weeks were diagnostic about your strategy and your pilots. This week is diagnostic about your *people system*. Six honest questions:

### 1. Do you actually know your AI talent inventory?

Not a HR-system list. Not LinkedIn searches. The actual humans inside your org who are doing AI work today — including the underground users (the analysts using Claude on their own initiative, the marketers using ChatGPT for first drafts, the engineers running Copilot).

If you don't know who they are, you can't build a community of practice and you can't scale what's working. If you know who they are but they're not connected to each other, you're losing the network effect.

The quick test: name three people in your org who are doing meaningful AI work outside the central team. If you can't, you have a visibility problem.

### 2. Where does your upskilling money actually go?

Pull the numbers. What did you spend on AI training in the last 12 months? Now break it down: what fraction went to AI-aware (broad), AI-fluent (managers), and AI-builder (technical) tiers?

If 80% went to building a small core of technical talent and 20% (or less) went to broad AI literacy, you're building capability that the rest of your organization can't pull from. Day 16's pattern. Most common failure mode.

### 3. What's your honest read on identity-layer change in affected roles?

Pick the role most affected by your most active AI initiative. Now answer: what is that role's job *actually* going to look like 18 months from now? Has anyone had that conversation explicitly with the people in that role?

If the answer is "we said it would augment their work" but you haven't shown them what the new job actually looks like, you're in for a rough adoption curve. Day 17 covers this.

### 4. Can you defend every AI system in production on the five ethics practices?

For each AI system you have in production, can you produce evidence on each of the five practices from Day 18: bias testing, transparency, data minimization, audit trails, kill switch?

This is the question that separates organizations that are seriously practicing AI ethics from those that have a committee. If you have to scramble to answer, schedule the work to close gaps before a regulator, customer, or auditor forces you to.

### 5. Are your AI teams actually triads — or sequential handoffs?

Look at your most active AI initiative. Is the team co-located (physically or virtually) with technical, domain, and product/operational depth on it daily? Or are domain experts pulled in for occasional reviews and product folks for status meetings?

The handoff pattern looks like collaboration but isn't. The triad pattern is harder to set up but produces dramatically better outcomes. Day 19's framework.

### 6. Do you have a Community of Practice — or just a Slack channel?

Tactical test from Day 20: name your community's founder, the cadence of its meetings, the artifacts it has accumulated, and the air cover it has from senior leadership. If you can't answer all four, you don't have a CoP yet — and that gap is constraining how much AI can spread organically inside your org.

## Common Patterns After Week 3

After this week's material, most leaders land in one of four places. Recognizing yours helps you focus the next four weeks.

**"We've been over-investing in the central team and under-investing in the rest."**
The most common pattern. You've built a strong AI team, shipped some pilots, and the rest of the organization is still in spectator mode. The fix is the broad AI-aware program (Day 16) and a Community of Practice (Day 20). It's cheap and high-impact, but it requires patience because the payoff is 6-12 months out.

**"We have the technology but not the change management."**
Your AI systems work. Adoption is poor. The cultural and identity work hasn't been done. This is the hardest pattern to fix because it requires uncomfortable conversations about what jobs look like in the new world. Day 17 is your starting point. Don't expect a quick fix; expect a 12-month investment in identity-layer change.

**"Our talent strategy is incoherent."**
You've been hiring opportunistically, training inconsistently, and tolerating wide variation in what "AI capability" means across teams. This is fixable but requires a written talent strategy (Day 15) and a discipline around upskilling investments (Day 16). The underlying decision: who do you want to be — a "buy from the market" company or a "build from within" company? Most orgs need to be hybrid, but they have to decide consciously.

**"We've been doing AI ethics theater."**
You have a committee. You have principles. You have a website page. But you can't produce evidence on the five practices for any system in production. This is more common than people admit, and it's becoming a serious legal and reputational risk as regulation tightens. Day 18 is your immediate priority. Treat this as a 90-day remediation project, not a strategic initiative.

## Your Move

Take 45 minutes and write a one-page **People Strategy Memo** for your organization's AI work. Structure it around the six audit questions above:

1. **Talent inventory** — who's doing AI work in our org today, and what's our gap?
2. **Upskilling investment** — what's our split across tiers, and what should it be?
3. **Change management** — for the most affected role, what is the new job and have we said so out loud?
4. **AI ethics evidence** — for our top three production systems, what's the state of the five practices?
5. **Team structure** — are our most active AI teams real triads, and if not, what changes?
6. **Community of Practice** — do we have one, and if not, when does it launch?

For each, answer in 2-3 sentences. End with three concrete commitments for the next 90 days.

Share this memo with the executive sponsor of your AI work and at least one HR partner. Their reactions will tell you which of the four patterns above you're in — and what specifically needs to happen next.

This document, combined with your Week 1 strategy brief and your Week 2 pilot charter, is the foundation for Week 4's portfolio-level thinking. Don't skip it.
